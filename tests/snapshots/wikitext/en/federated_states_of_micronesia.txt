The '''Federated States of Micronesia''' ({{IPAc-en|audio=en-us-Micronesia.ogg|ˌ|m|aɪ|k|r|oʊ|ˈ|n|iː|ʒ|ə}}, abbreviated '''FSM'''), or simply '''Micronesia''', is an [[island country]] in [[Micronesia]], a region of [[Oceania]]. The [[federation]] encompasses the majority of the [[Caroline Islands]] (excluding [[Palau]]) and consists of four [[Administrative divisions of the Federated States of Micronesia#States|states]]—from west to east: [[Yap State|Yap]], [[Chuuk State|Chuuk]], [[Pohnpei State|Pohnpei]], and [[Kosrae]]—that span the western [[Pacific]] just north of the [[equator]] for a [[Longitude|longitudinal]] distance of almost {{cvt|2700|km|mi}}. Together, the states comprise around 607 islands and a combined [[land area]] of approximately {{convert|702|km2|abbr=on|disp=or}}.

The entire island federation lies across the northern Pacific accordingly: northeast of [[Indonesia]] and [[Papua New Guinea]], south of [[Guam]] and the [[Marianas]], west of [[Nauru]] and the [[Marshall Islands]], east of [[Palau]] and the [[Philippines]], about {{cvt|2900|km|mi}} north of eastern [[Australia]], {{cvt|3,400|km|mi}} southeast of Japan, and some {{cvt|4000|km|0}} southwest of [[Honolulu]] of the [[Hawaiian Islands]].

The country's total land area is relatively small, but its waters occupy nearly {{convert|3|e6km2|e6mi2|abbr=unit}} of the [[Pacific Ocean]], giving the country the 14th-largest [[exclusive economic zone]] in the world.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.economist.com/graphic-detail/2016/01/13/drops-in-the-ocean-frances-marine-territories?fsrc=scn%2Ffb%2Fwl%2Fbl%2Fdc%2Fst%2Fdropsintheocean|title=Drops in the ocean: France's marine territories|date=January 13, 2016|newspaper=The Economist|access-date=January 28, 2021|archive-date=February 2, 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210202185124/https://www.economist.com/graphic-detail/2016/01/13/drops-in-the-ocean-frances-marine-territories?fsrc=scn%2Ffb%2Fwl%2Fbl%2Fdc%2Fst%2Fdropsintheocean|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.seaaroundus.org/data/#/eez/583 |work=seaaroundus.org |title=Micronesia (Federated States of) |access-date=March 3, 2023 |archive-date=March 6, 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230306031152/http://www.seaaroundus.org/data/#/eez/583 |url-status=live }}</ref> The nation's capital is [[Palikir]], on [[Pohnpei Island]], and its largest city is [[Weno]], an island municipality in the [[Chuuk Lagoon]].

Each of its four states is centered on one or more main [[volcanic island]]s, and all but Kosrae include numerous outlying [[atoll]]s. The FSM spreads across part of the [[Caroline Islands]] in the wider region of [[Micronesia]], which region consists of thousands of small islands divided among several countries. The term ''Micronesia'' may refer to the Federated States of Micronesia or to the region as a whole.

The FSM was a part of the former [[Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands]] (TTPI), a [[United Nations]] Trust Territory administered by the [[United States]] from 1947 to 1994. On May 10, 1979, the islands ratified a constitutional government and then became a sovereign state after attaining independence on November 3, 1986—under a [[Compact of Free Association]] with the United States. Other neighboring island entities (also former members of the TTPI), also formed constitutional governments, becoming the [[Marshall Islands|Republic of the Marshall Islands]] and the [[Palau|Republic of Palau]]. The FSM has a seat in the United Nations and has been a member of the [[Pacific Community]] since 1983.

== History ==
{{Main|History of the Federated States of Micronesia}}

=== Early settlement and Nan Madol ===
The ancestors of the [[Micronesians]] settled over four thousand years ago.<ref>{{Cite web |title=About Federated States of Micronesia – Federated States of Micronesia |url=https://visit-micronesia.fm/about-fsm/ |access-date=March 29, 2025 |language=en-US}}</ref> A decentralized chieftain-based system eventually evolved into a more centralized economic and religious culture centered on [[Yap Island]].<ref name=":4">{{Cite web |date=February 3, 2012 |title=Federated States of Micronesia |url=https://www.pacificrisa.org/places/federated-states-of-micronesia/ |access-date=March 29, 2025 |website=Pacific RISA - Managing Climate Risk in Pacific Islands |language=en-US}}</ref>

[[Nan Madol]], a [[UNESCO]] [[List of World Heritage Sites in The Federated States of Micronesia|World Heritage Site]], consisting of a series of small [[artificial island]]s linked by a network of canals, is often called the Venice of the Pacific. It is located on the eastern periphery of the island of Pohnpei and used to be the ceremonial and political seat of the [[Saudeleur dynasty]] that united Pohnpei's estimated 25,000 people from about [[Anno Domini|AD]] 500 until 1500 when the centralized system collapsed.<ref name=":4" />

=== European contact and colonial rule ===
[[File:Reception of the Manila Galleon by the Chamorro in the Ladrones Islands, ca. 1590.jpg|thumb|left|[[Manila galleon|Manila Galleon]] in the [[Mariana Islands|Marianas]] and [[Caroline Islands|Carolinas]], {{Circa|1590}} ''[[Boxer Codex]]'']]

European explorers—first the [[Portugal|Portuguese]] in search of the [[Maluku Islands|Spice Islands]] ([[Indonesia]]) and then the [[Spanish Empire|Spanish]]—reached the [[Caroline Islands|Carolines]] in the sixteenth century. The [[Treaty of Tordesillas]] gave these lands to [[Spain]] and the Spanish incorporated the archipelago to the [[Spanish East Indies]] through the capital, [[Manila]], and in the 19th century established several outposts and missions. In 1887, they founded the town of ''Santiago de la Ascensión'' in what today is [[Kolonia]] on the island of Pohnpei.

In the 1870s, Germany began extending its sphere of influence in the Caroline Islands, leading to the [[Carolines Question]] of 1885 in which [[Pope Leo XIII]] was asked to determine if Germany or Spain had authority over the islands. The result was a confirmation of Spanish authority over the islands, but Germany would have free access to the islands.<ref name="Bjorge2014">{{cite book|author=Eirik Bjorge|title=The Evolutionary Interpretation of Treaties|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=hWcHBAAAQBAJ&pg=PA149|date=July 17, 2014|publisher=OUP Oxford|isbn=978-0-19-102576-1|page=149|access-date=December 21, 2022|archive-date=July 19, 2023|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230719062219/https://books.google.com/books?id=hWcHBAAAQBAJ&pg=PA149|url-status=live}}</ref>

Following defeat in the [[Spanish–American War]], the Spanish sold the archipelago to [[German Empire|Germany]] in 1899 under the [[German–Spanish Treaty (1899)|German–Spanish Treaty of 1899]]. Germany incorporated it into [[German New Guinea]]. (A few remote islands, notably [[Kapingamarangi]], were not specifically named in the treaty, but this remained unnoticed until the late 1940s and, while acknowledging the historical curiosity in 1949, Spain has made no modern claims to the islands.{{sfn|Pastor y Santos|1950|p=21}}<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/countries/micronesia-federated-states-of/#transnational-issues|title=Micronesia, Federated States of|work=CIA World Factbook|access-date=July 24, 2021|archive-date=July 22, 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210722221127/https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/countries/micronesia-federated-states-of/#transnational-issues|url-status=dead}}</ref>)

During [[World War I]], it was captured by [[Empire of Japan|Japan]]. Following the war, the [[League of Nations]] awarded a [[League of Nations Mandate|mandate]] for Japan to administer the islands as part of the [[South Seas Mandate]].<ref name=":5">Foster, S., Hezel, F.X. "Micronesia." ''Encyclopedia Britannica'', February 27, 2025. https://www.britannica.com/place/Micronesia-republic-Pacific-Ocean</ref>

=== World War II and the aftermath ===

==== Operation Hailstone ====
[[Chuuk Lagoon|Truk Lagoon]] served as a significant base for the [[Imperial Japanese Navy]] during [[World War II]]. In February 1944, the [[United States Navy|U.S. Navy]] launched [[Operation Hailstone]], a massive air and surface attack on Truk Lagoon. Over two days, U.S. carrier aircraft and surface vessels targeted Japanese warships, merchant vessels, and shore installations, resulting in the sinking of numerous ships and the destruction of hundreds of aircraft. This operation severely diminished Japan's naval capabilities in the Pacific.<ref>Hornfischer, James (2016). ''The Fleet at Flood Tide: America at Total War in the Pacific, 1944–1945''. Random House Publishing Group</ref><ref>{{Cite book |last=Prados |first=John |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=l9NNPgAACAAJ |title=Combined Fleet Decoded: The Secret History of American Intelligence and the Japanese Navy in World War II |date=2001 |publisher=Naval Institute Press |isbn=978-1-55750-431-9 |language=en}}</ref>

==== U.S. administration under the United Nations ====
Following World War II, the islands of Micronesia were placed under the administration of the United States as part of the [[Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands]]. This arrangement was formalized on 2 April 1947, with the adoption of [[United Nations Security Council Resolution 21]], which approved the terms of [[trustee]]ship for the Pacific Islands formerly under Japanese mandate.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Security Council Resolution 21 - UNSCR |url=https://unscr.com/en/resolutions/21 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20250312104346/http://unscr.com/en/resolutions/21 |archive-date=March 12, 2025 |access-date=2025-03-29 |website=unscr.com |language=en |url-status=live }}</ref><ref>United Nations Security Council. (1947). Trusteeship of Strategic Areas (Resolution No. 21)</ref>

=== Path to independence ===
On May 10, 1979, four of the Trust Territory districts [[1978 Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands constitutional referendum|ratified]] a new [[Constitution of the Federated States of Micronesia|constitution]] to become the Federated States of Micronesia. [[Palau]], the [[Marshall Islands]], and the [[Northern Mariana Islands]] chose not to participate. The FSM signed a [[Compact of Free Association]] with the United States, which entered into force on November 3, 1986, marking Micronesia's emergence from trusteeship to independence. Independence was formally concluded under international law in 1990, when the United Nations officially ended the Trusteeship status pursuant to [[United Nations Security Council Resolution 683|Security Council Resolution 683]]. The Compact was renewed in 2004.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.fsmlaw.org/compact/|title=Compact of Free Association|access-date=2 January 2016|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160126050555/http://www.fsmlaw.org/compact/|archive-date=26 January 2016}}</ref><ref name=":5" /><ref>{{Cite web |date=2015-06-11 |title=Federated States of Micronesia {{!}} U.S. Department of the Interior |url=https://www.doi.gov/oia/islands/fsm |access-date=2025-03-29 |website=www.doi.gov |language=en}}</ref>

In February 2021, due to a bitter leadership dispute, the Federated States of Micronesia announced it would quit the [[Pacific Islands Forum]] in its formal process of withdrawal. However, in June 2022, the Suva Agreement was reached and the Federated States of Micronesia agreed to remain in the forum.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Dziedzic |first=Stephen |date=2022-06-07 |title=Pacific leaders strike a deal to keep Micronesian nations from splitting with Pacific Islands Forum |url=https://www.abc.net.au/news/2022-06-07/pacific-leaders-strike-deal-pacific-islands-forum/101133026 |access-date=2024-11-02 |work=ABC News |language=en-AU}}</ref><ref name=":5" />

==Politics==
{{main|Politics of the Federated States of Micronesia}}

The Federated States of Micronesia is governed by the [[Constitution of the Federated States of Micronesia|1979 constitution]], which guarantees fundamental human rights and establishes a separation of governmental powers. This constitution structures the national government to be similar to—but not exactly like—that of the United States. The unicameral [[Congress of the Federated States of Micronesia|Congress]] has fourteen members elected by popular vote. Four senators—one from each state—serve four-year terms; the remaining ten senators represent single-member districts based on population and serve two-year terms. Congress elects the [[President of the Federated States of Micronesia|President]] and [[Vice President of the Federated States of Micronesia|Vice President]] from among the four state-based senators to serve four-year terms in the executive branch. Their congressional seats are then filled by special elections.

An appointed cabinet supports the president and vice president. There are no formal political parties.

===Defense and foreign affairs===
{{Further|Compact of Free Association|Foreign relations of the Federated States of Micronesia}}
[[File:US Navy 080831-N-8878B-345 An MH-60S Sea Hawk helicopter flies over the waters of Micronesia.jpg|thumb|left|[[Sikorsky SH-60 Seahawk|Sea Hawk]] helicopter (US Navy) flies over the waters of Chuuk, Micronesia.]]

In international politics, the Federated States of Micronesia has often voted with the United States concerning [[United Nations General Assembly]] resolutions.<ref>[https://2009-2017.state.gov/documents/organization/82642.pdf General Assembly - Overall Votes - Comparison with U.S. vote] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191202183404/https://2009-2017.state.gov/documents/organization/82642.pdf |date=December 2, 2019 }}  lists Micronesia as in the country with the fourth high coincidence of votes. Micronesia has always been in the top four.</ref>

[[File:FSS Tosiwo Nakayama at Austal shipyards in Henderson, Western Australia, November 2021 03.jpg|thumb|The FSS ''Tosiwo Nakayama'', a [[Guardian-class patrol boat]] of the Federated States of Micronesia]]

The FSM is a sovereign, self-governing state in [[Associated state|free association]] with the [[United States|United States of America]], which is wholly responsible for its defense. The [[FSM National Police]] operates a Maritime Wing Unit. The Compact of Free Association allows [[nationality law of the Federated States of Micronesia|FSM citizens]] to join the U.S. military without having to obtain U.S. permanent residency or citizenship,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.us-army-info.com/pages/pdfs/enlistment_standards.pdf|archive-url=https://wayback.archive-it.org/all/20081001231147/http://www.us-army-info.com/pages/pdfs/enlistment_standards.pdf|url-status=dead|archive-date=2008-10-01|title=U.S. Military Enlistment Standards}}</ref> allows for immigration and employment for Micronesians in the U.S., and establishes economic and technical aid programs.

==Geography==
{{main|Geography of the Federated States of Micronesia}}

{{more citations needed section|date=March 2023}}
[[File:Koloniasokehs.jpg|thumb|upright=1.35|A view of Kolonia Town from Sokehs Ridge in [[Pohnpei]]]]

The Federated States of Micronesia consists of 607 [[island]]s extending {{convert|2900|km|0|abbr=on}} across the [[archipelago]] of the [[Caroline Islands]] east of the [[Philippines]]. The islands have a combined area of {{convert|702|km2|0|abbr=on}}.<ref name=":0" />

The islands are grouped into four states, which are [[Yap State|Yap]], [[Chuuk State|Chuuk]] (called Truk until January 1990), [[Pohnpei State|Pohnpei]] (known as "Ponape" until November 1984), and [[Kosrae]] (formerly Kusaie).<ref>{{Cite book|last=Keesing|first=Roger M.|title=Melanesian Pidgin and the oceanic substrate|date=1988|publisher=Stanford University Press|isbn=0-8047-1450-9|location=Stanford, Calif.|pages=15|oclc=17383715}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|title=The Europa world year book 2004.|date=2004|publisher=Europa|isbn=1-85743-253-3|location=London|oclc=55795909}}</ref> These four states are each represented by a white star on the national flag. The capital is [[Palikir]], on Pohnpei.

=== Biodiversity ===
The Federated States of Micronesia (FSM) is recognized as a biodiversity hotspot, hosting approximately 3,025 animal species and 1,553 plant species.<ref>{{Cite journal |last1=Park |first1=Myung Soo |last2=Yoo |first2=Shinnam |last3=Cho |first3=Yoonhee |last4=Park |first4=Ki Hyeong |last5=Kim |first5=Nam Kyu |last6=Lee |first6=Hyi-Seung |last7=Lim |first7=Young Woon |date=2021 |title=Investigation of the Fungal Diversity of the Federated States of Micronesia and the Construction of an Updated Fungal Inventory |journal=Mycobiology |volume=49 |issue=6 |pages=551–558 |doi=10.1080/12298093.2021.2012327 |issn=1229-8093 |pmc=8725818 |pmid=35035247}}</ref> The diversity of terrestrial plants and animals within the FSM varies from east to west due to differences in climate, geology, topography, and geographical isolation.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Terrestrial Biodiversity of FSM - Dataset - Pacific Data Hub |url=https://pacificdata.org/data/dataset/terrestrial-biodiversity-of-fsm3eb73467-e1cf-483f-83f2-6f7b68be5879 |access-date=2025-03-29 |website=pacificdata.org |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Terrestrial Biodiversity of the FSM |url=https://www.sprep.org/att/IRC/eCOPIES/Countries/FSM/17.pdf}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Federated States of Micronesia Environment Data Portal - Organisations - Pacific Data Hub |url=https://pacificdata.org/data/organization/fsm-data?license_id=sprep-public-license&member_countries=fm&tags=flora-and-fauna |access-date=2025-03-29 |website=pacificdata.org |language=en}}</ref>

Over 457 plant species have been introduced to the islands of the FSM by [[Micronesians|early Micronesians]] and subsequent settlers.<ref name=":6">{{Cite web |title=Convention on biological diversity |url=https://www.cbd.int/doc/world/fm/fm-nr-01-en.pdf}}</ref> The FSM comprises 607 islands extending 2,900&nbsp;km across the [[Caroline Islands]], with a combined area of 702&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>. The FSM has 58 [[Key Biodiversity Area]]s.<ref name=":6" />

The region is characterized by thousands of isolated small coral atolls and higher volcanic islands, contributing to its high biodiversity.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Federated States of Micronesia (sprep) |url=https://www.sprep.org/att/IRC/eCOPIES/Countries/FSM/66.pdf}}</ref> The FSM's National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan outlines efforts to protect and conserve its rich biodiversity.<ref>{{Cite web |title=FSM National Biodiversity Strategy |url=https://www.theprif.org/sites/theprif.org/files/2020-08/FSM%20NBSAP%20-%2026%20Oct%202018.pdf}}</ref> The FSM's marine biodiversity is under pressure due to growing population demands, leading to increased degradation of marine resources.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Marine Biodiversity of the FSM |url=https://www.comfsm.fm/bchm/MarineBio.pdf}}</ref>

=== Climate ===
[[File:Operation Christmas Drop 16 161207-F-IX728-306.jpg|300px|thumb|[[Satawal]] island, Yap State]]
The Federated States of Micronesia has a [[tropical rainforest climate]] ([[Köppen climate classification|Köppen]]: ''Af''). The weather is warm, humid and rainy all year round. The islands are located north of the equator and are affected by constant trade winds, which temper the climate. Minimum temperatures range all year round between 22 and 25&nbsp;°C, and maximum temperatures between 30 and 32&nbsp;°C.<ref>{{Cite web |title=That's how warm it is in the Federated States of Micronesia: 30.6 °C on average per year and over 1750 hours of sunshine! |url=https://www.worlddata.info/oceania/micronesia/climate.php |access-date=2025-03-29 |website=Worlddata.info |language=en}}</ref> The abundant precipitations oscillate between 2500 and 5000&nbsp;mm per year, although in the faces oriented to the [[wind]] they can surpass 6000&nbsp;mm. Mount Nahnalaud, only 750 m high, on the island of Pohnpei, receives an average of 10,160&nbsp;mm, being one of the rainiest places on earth, with almost always overcast skies.<ref name=":7">{{Cite web |title=Micronesia climate: average weather, temperature, rain - Climates to Travel |url=https://www.climatestotravel.com/climate/micronesia |access-date=2025-03-29 |website=www.climatestotravel.com}}</ref> In general, the rains are produced by showers and storms of short duration but very intense. The driest places are the flat atolls, where rainfall can drop below 3,000&nbsp;mm. The driest months are January and February, with no less than 250&nbsp;mm and 20 days of rain.<ref name=":7" />

Climate patterns in the FSM are also affected by the [[El Niño–Southern Oscillation|El Niño-Southern Oscillation]], leading to considerable variability in weather conditions from year to year. During [[El Niño–Southern Oscillation|El Niño]] events, the region may experience increased rainfall and storm activity, while [[El Niño–Southern Oscillation|La Niña]] events can bring drier conditions and potential droughts.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Current and Future climate of the FSM |url=https://www.pacificclimatechangescience.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/7_PACCSAP-FSM-11pp_WEB.pdf}}</ref>

==Economy==
{{main|Economy of the Federated States of Micronesia}}

[[File:Fishing at Chuuk, Micronesia in 1931.png|thumb|Fishing in Chuuk, 1931]]
Economic activity in the Federated States of Micronesia consists primarily of [[subsistence farming]] and [[fishing]]. The islands have few mineral deposits worth exploiting, except for high-grade [[phosphate]]. [[longline fishing|Long line]] fishing of [[tuna]] is also viable with foreign vessels from [[China]] that operated in the 1990s. The potential for a [[tourist industry]] exists, but the remoteness of the location and a lack of adequate facilities hinder development. [[United States foreign aid|Financial assistance from the U.S.]] is the primary source of revenue, with the U.S. pledged to spend $1.3 billion in the islands in 1986–2001; when the Compact was amended in 2004, the United States committed to providing $110 million in development aid through 2023.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://2009-2017.state.gov/r/pa/ei/bgn/1839.htm|title=US Relations with the Federated States of Micronesia|publisher=United States Department of State|access-date=May 22, 2019|archive-date=June 4, 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190604190535/https://2009-2017.state.gov/r/pa/ei/bgn/1839.htm|url-status=live}}</ref> The [[The World Factbook|CIA World Factbook]] lists high dependence on U.S. aid as one of the main concerns of the FSM.<ref name=":0" /> Geographical isolation and a poorly developed infrastructure are major impediments to long-term growth.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.un.org/special-rep/ohrlls/sid/SIDS-states/profiles/fed_st_micronesia.htm |title=Federated States of Micronesia |publisher=United Nations |access-date=17 November 2012 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121130043513/http://www.un.org/special-rep/ohrlls/sid/SIDS-states/profiles/fed_st_micronesia.htm |archive-date=November 30, 2012 }}</ref>

== Society ==

===Demographics===
{{main|Demographics of the Federated States of Micronesia}}

[[File:Ulithi-Dancers.jpg|thumb|upright=1.1|People performing a welcome ceremony on the [[Ulithi]] atoll]]
The indigenous population of the nation, which is predominantly [[Micronesians|Micronesian]], consists of various ethnolinguistic groups. It has a nearly 100% Pacific Islander and [[Asian people|Asian]] population: [[Chuukese people|Chuukese]] 48.8%, [[Pohnpeian language|Pohnpeian]] 24.2%, [[Kosraean language|Kosraean]] 6.2%, [[Yapese people|Yapese]] 5.2%, [[Yap]] outer islands 4.5%, [[Asian ethnic groups|Asian]] 1.8%, [[Polynesians|Polynesian]] 1.5%, other 6.4%, unknown 1.4%. A sizable minority also have some [[Japanese settlement in the Federated States of Micronesia|Japanese ancestry]], which is a result of intermarriages between Japanese settlers and Micronesians during the Japanese colonial period.<ref>[http://www.fsmgov.org/press/pr12120a.htm President Emanuel Mori Meets With Japan Prime Minister Yasuo Fukuda] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150924015858/http://www.fsmgov.org/press/pr12120a.htm|date=September 24, 2015}}; [http://www.aesonline.org/3056 AESonline.org] {{webarchive|url=https://archive.today/20070616091249/http://www.aesonline.org/3056|date=June 16, 2007}} Government of the Federated States of Micronesia, December 12, 2007</ref>

There is also a growing expatriate population of Americans, Australians, Europeans, and residents from China and the Philippines since the 1990s. [[English language|English]] has become the common language of the government, and for secondary and tertiary education. Outside of the main capital towns of the four FSM states, the local languages are primarily spoken. In the Catholic mission of [[Pohnpei]], among the Mercedarian missionaries, considered an institution in the country, [[Spanish language|Spanish]] is also spoken. Growth remains high at more than 3% annually, offset somewhat by net emigration.

===Religion===
{{main|Religion in the Federated States of Micronesia}}

[[File:Catholic Belltower, Kolonia.jpg|thumb|upright=1.1|Cathedral of Ponape Belltower, in Kolonia, on the island of Pohnpei, built in 1909 by German Capuchin missionaries]]
The people of the Federated States of Micronesia are 97% [[Christians|Christian]].<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=https://www.state.gov/reports/2019-report-on-international-religious-freedom/micronesia/|title="2019 Report on International Religious Freedom: Micronesia". United States Department of State.|access-date=October 28, 2022|archive-date=March 26, 2023|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230326125307/https://www.state.gov/reports/2019-report-on-international-religious-freedom/micronesia/|url-status=live}}</ref> More than half of the population follows the Catholic Church (55%)<ref name=":1" /> and about 42%<ref name=":1" /> follow various Protestant Christian groups. In general this is due to [[Spanish Empire|Spanish]] and German colonial history. Spanish rule meant that a large part of the population remained Catholic. During the [[German Empire|German]] colonial period, until 1914, Catholic and Protestant missionaries from the German Empire were deployed. Several [[Protestant]] denominations, as well as the [[Roman Catholic (term)|Roman]] [[Catholic Church]], are present in every Micronesian state.<ref name="report">[https://2001-2009.state.gov/g/drl/rls/irf/2007/90145.htm International Religious Freedom Report 2007: Micronesia, Federated States of] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200731054843/https://2001-2009.state.gov/g/drl/rls/irf/2007/90145.htm |date=July 31, 2020}}. United States [[Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights and Labor]] (September 14, 2007). ''This article incorporates text from this source, which is in the [[public domain]].''</ref> Most Protestant groups trace their roots to American Congregationalist missionaries.<ref name=report/> On the island of Kosrae, the population is approximately 7,800; 95 percent are Protestants.<ref name=report/> On [[Pohnpei]], the population of 35,000 is evenly divided between Protestants and Catholics. Most immigrants are Filipino Catholics who have joined local Catholic churches, e.g. Our Lady of Mercy Catholic Church in Pohnpei.<ref name=report/>

On Chuuk and Yap, an estimated 60 percent are Catholic and 40 percent are Protestant.<ref name=report/> Religious groups with small followings include [[Baptists]], [[Assemblies of God]], [[Salvation Army]], [[Seventh-day Adventists]], [[Jehovah's Witnesses]], [[The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints]] (Mormons), and the [[Baháʼí Faith]].<ref name=report/> There is a small group of [[Buddhists]] on Pohnpei,<ref name=report/> and a small group of [[Ahmadiyya|Ahmadiyya Muslims]] in both Pohnpei and Kosrae. Attendance at religious services is generally high; [[church (building)|churches]] are well supported by their congregations and play a significant role in civil society.<ref name=report/>

In the 1890s, on the island of Pohnpei, intermissionary conflicts and the conversion of clan leaders resulted in religious divisions along clan lines which persist today.<ref name=report/> More Protestants live on the western side of the island, while more Catholics live on the eastern side.<ref name=report/> [[Missionaries]] of many religious traditions are present and operate freely.<ref name=report/> The Constitution provides for [[freedom of religion]], and the Government generally respects this right in practice.<ref name=report/> The US government received no reports of societal abuses or discrimination based on religious belief or practice in 2007.<ref name=report/>

==Culture==
[[File:Yap Stone Money.jpg|thumb|upright=1.1|A large (approximately 2.4&nbsp;m or about 8&nbsp;ft in height) example of Yapese stone money ([[Rai stones]]) in the village of Gachpar]]
Each of the four states has its own culture and traditions, but there are also common cultural and economic bonds that are centuries old. Cultural similarities include the importance of the traditional extended family and clan systems and are found on all the islands.

The island of Yap is notable for its "stone money" ([[Rai stones]]), large disks usually of calcite, up to {{convert|4|m|ft|sp=us}} in diameter, with a hole in the middle. The islanders, aware of the owner of a piece, do not necessarily move them when ownership changes. There are five major types: ''Mmbul'', ''Gaw'', ''Ray'', ''Yar'', and ''Reng'', the last being only {{convert|30|cm|0|abbr=on}} in diameter. Their value is based on both size and history, many of them having been brought from other islands, as far as [[New Guinea]], but most coming in ancient times from [[Palau]]. Approximately 6,500 of them are scattered around the island.

Pohnpei is home to ''[[Nan Madol|Nan Madol: Ceremonial Centre of Eastern Micronesia]]'', a [[UNESCO]] [[World Heritage Site]], but the site is currently listed as ''In Danger'' due to natural causes.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/1503|title=Nan Madol: Ceremonial Centre of Eastern Micronesia - UNESCO World Heritage Centre|date=7 August 2018|website=UNESCO World Heritage Centre|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180807221707/https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/1503|archive-date=7 August 2018|url-status=live|access-date=7 August 2018}}</ref> The government is working on the conservation of the site.

===Music===
{{Main|Music and dances of the Federated States of Micronesia}}

[[File:Yapese men dancers in traditional dress celebrating Yap Day.jpg|thumb|Yapese men dancing in traditional dress]]
[[File:Ponape Handicraft Shop.jpg|thumb|A shop in Pohnpei selling traditional souvenirs]]
Traditional dances on the main islands includes "stick dancing" on [[Pohnpei]], [[Chuuk State|Chuuk]] and [[Yap]], standing dances on [[Chuuk State|Chuuk]] and sitting dances on Yap<ref name=dance>{{cite web|work=Jane's Oceania Page|url=http://www.janesoceania.com/micronesia_music_anthology/|access-date=September 27, 2005 |title=Micronesia Music Anthology|url-status=usurped|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20060101073112/http://www.janesoceania.com/micronesia_music_anthology/|archive-date=January 1, 2006}}</ref> and [[Chuuk State|Chuuk]]. The Yapese are particularly known for their skills in dancing. The Yapese stick dance is performed by men, women and children together, while standing dances are performed either by women or men and boys, but never both together. The men participate in various dancing competitions, which are segregated by [[caste]]; the lower castes have some distinct dances, such as a woman's standing dance, but can only dance when authorized by a person of a higher caste.<ref name=Yap>{{cite web|work=Jane's Oceania Page|url=http://www.janesoceania.com/micronesia_yap/index.htm|access-date=September 27, 2005 |title=Aspects of Yap|url-status=usurped|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20050227045237/http://www.janesoceania.com/micronesia_yap/index.htm|archive-date=February 27, 2005}}</ref>

===Literature===
There have been very few published literary writers from the Federated States of Micronesia.<ref>[http://guam.mvarietynews.com/index.php?view=article&id=4591%3Aseeking-micronesian-literary-writers&option=com_content&Itemid=2 "Seeking Micronesian literary writers"], ''Marianas Variety'', February 18, 2009 {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140928150350/http://guam.mvarietynews.com/index.php?view=article&id=4591%3Aseeking-micronesian-literary-writers&option=com_content&Itemid=2|date=September 28, 2014}}</ref> In 2008, [[Emelihter Kihleng]] became the first ever Micronesian to publish a collection of poetry in the English language.<ref>[http://www.doi.gov/oia/press/2008/05122008b.html "Micronesian Poet Publishes Collection of Poems"], Office of Insular Affairs, May 12, 2008 {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120229151437/http://www.doi.gov/oia/press/2008/05122008b.html|date=February 29, 2012}}</ref>
