|url-status= live }}</ref> It is part of the [[Windward Islands]] chain in the [[Lesser Antilles]] archipelago in the [[Caribbean Sea]]. The capital, [[Roseau]], is located on the western side of the island. Dominica's closest neighbours are two [[Special member state territories and the European Union|constituent territories]] of the [[European Union]], both overseas departments of France: [[Guadeloupe]] to the northwest and [[Martinique]] to the south-southeast. Dominica comprises a land area of {{Convert|750|km2|abbr=on}}, and the highest point is [[Morne Diablotins]], at {{Convert|1448|m|abbr=on}} in elevation. The population was 71,293 at the 2011 census.<ref name="2011 census"/>

The island was settled by the [[Arawak]] arriving from South America in the fifth century. The [[Kalinago]] displaced the Arawak by the 15th century. [[Christopher Columbus]] is said to have passed the island on Sunday, 3 November 1493. It was later colonised by Europeans, predominantly by the French from the 1690s to 1763. The French trafficked slaves from West Africa to Dominica to work on coffee plantations. [[Kingdom of Great Britain|Great Britain]] took possession in 1763 after the [[Seven Years' War]], and gradually established English as its official language. The island gained independence as a republic in 1978.

Dominica has been nicknamed the "Nature Island of the Caribbean" for its natural environment.<ref>{{cite book |url= https://www.amazon.co.uk/Dominica-Nature-Island-Caribbean-Hansib/dp/1870518179 |title= Dominica: Nature Island of the Caribbean |last1= Evans |first1= P. C. |first2= L.| last2= Honychurch| authorlink2=Lennox Honychurch |year= 1989 |publisher= Hansib |archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20210424132805/https://www.amazon.co.uk/Dominica-Nature-Island-Caribbean-Hansib/dp/1870518179 |archive-date= 24 April 2021 |url-status= dead |access-date= }}</ref> It is the youngest island in the Lesser Antilles, and is still being formed by [[Geothermal activity|geothermal]]-[[volcanic]] activity, as evidenced by the world's second-largest [[hot spring]], called [[Boiling Lake]]. The island has lush mountainous [[rainforests]] and is the home of many rare plants, animals, and bird species. There are [[xeric|dry shrubland]] areas in some of the western coastal regions, but heavy rainfall occurs inland. The [[sisserou parrot]], also known as the imperial amazon, is [[critically endangered]] and found only on Dominica. It is the island's national bird and is featured on the [[Flag of Dominica|national flag]], making Dominica one of two sovereign nations whose official flag features the color purple.<ref>{{cite web |title=Country Flags With Purple |url= https://www.worldatlas.com/articles/country-flags-with-purple.html |website=World Atlas |date=18 December 2018 |access-date=3 April 2019 |archive-date=31 May 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200531012907/https://www.worldatlas.com/articles/country-flags-with-purple.html |url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|date=2017-11-20|title=The Real Reason Purple Isn't Used on Many National Flags|url=https://tiphero.com/purple-flags|access-date=2021-11-29|website=TipHero|language=en-US|archive-date=7 April 2022|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220407235559/https://tiphero.com/purple-flags|url-status=live}}</ref> The country is a member of the [[Commonwealth of Nations]], the [[United Nations]], the [[Organization of American States]], the [[Organisation internationale de la Francophonie]], the [[Organisation of Eastern Caribbean States]] and the [[Non-Aligned Movement]].

== Etymology ==
The [[Kalinago]] called the island ''Wai‘tu kubuli'', which means "Tall is her body", likely referring to the island personified as a goddess (comparable to [[Gaia]]).<ref>{{cite web |title=Discover Dominica: an introduction to our Caribbean island |url=http://www.dominica.dm/site/dominica.cfm |publisher= | website= dominica.dm |url-status= dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100923082054/http://dominica.dm/site/dominica.cfm |archive-date=23 September 2010 |access-date=2010-06-27}}</ref>

[[Christopher Columbus]], sailing for [[Spain]], named the island Dominica, an abbreviation of the [[Latin language|Latin]] {{lang|la|[[wikt:dies Dominicus|dies Dominica]]}} (meaning "day of the Lord", that is, Sunday), because he first sighted the landmass on a Sunday in November 1493.<ref name= "Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica" /> In keeping with the Spanish pronunciation of its name,<ref name="pronunciation_stuff">{{cite web| url= https://www.stuff.co.nz/travel/news/76397895/the-22-places-youre-probably-pronouncing-incorrectly|title=The 22 places you're probably pronouncing incorrectly|date=29 January 2016|first=David|last=Whitley|website=[[Stuff (website)|Stuff]]|access-date=2020-08-31|archive-date=8 February 2022|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20220208014714/https://www.stuff.co.nz/travel/news/76397895/the-22-places-youre-probably-pronouncing-incorrectly|url-status=live}}</ref> Dominica's name is pronounced with emphasis on the second ''i''.<ref name="pronunciation_guardian" /><ref name="pronunciation_virtual" /> 

The similar names and the identical [[demonym]] with the [[Dominican Republic]] have caused some in Dominica to advocate for a name change, which they say would contribute to further establishing the local identity.<ref>{{cite web | first= Dian |last= Rolle |title=COMMENTARY: It's time for a new identity |url=https://dominicanewsonline.com/news/homepage/commentary-its-time-for-a-new-identity/ |access-date=May 29, 2023 |work=Dominica News Online |date=May 4, 2021}}</ref>

== History ==
{{main|History of Dominica}}

=== Geologic history ===
Dominica first emerged from the sea during the [[Oligocene]] Epoch approximately 27 million years ago, making it one of the last Caribbean islands to be formed by [[Volcanism|volcanic activity]].{{citation needed|date=June 2024}}

=== Pre-colonial period and early European contact ===

Dominica's precolonial Indigenous inhabitants were the [[Kalinago]] people, who are thought to have driven out the previous [[Arawak]] population.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica">{{cite web |url=https://www.britannica.com/place/Dominica |title= Dominica |work= [[Encyclopedia Britannica]] | access-date=29 June 2019 |archive-date=12 October 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231012123836/https://www.britannica.com/place/Dominica |url-status=live }}</ref> However, this may be a partial misconception as ancestral Kalinago narratives mention the two groups coexisting and intermarrying. The Arawak were not the primary Indigenous group. It is believed that before them were a group of paleo-Indians who arrived towards the end of the late Pleistocene period.{{Citation needed|date=May 2025}}

In 1493, [[Christopher Columbus]] first spotted the island during his [[Voyages of Christopher Columbus|second voyage]] to the Americas. Because he saw the island on a Sunday (November 3, 1493), Columbus named the island Dominica (Latin for 'Sunday').<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> Some Spanish colonisers settled there. But, as European explorers and settlers entered the region, Indigenous refugees from surrounding islands settled in Dominica and pushed out the Spanish settlers. The Spanish instead settled other areas that were easier to control.{{citation needed|date=June 2025}}

=== French colony ===
{{Main|French West Indies}}

Spain had little success in colonising Dominica. In 1632, the French [[Compagnie des Îles de l'Amérique]] claimed it and other ''"Petites Antilles"'' for [[Kingdom of France|France]], but no physical occupation took place.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> Between 1642 and 1650, French missionary [[Raymond Breton]] became the first regular European visitor to the island.<ref>{{cite web |author1-link=20 April 2021 |title=Seen British Passport Dominica State? Learn about British Passport Dominica State |url=https://www.bprol.com/en/2020/10/23/british-dominica-passport/ |website=bprol.com |access-date=22 March 2026 |url-status=live |archive-date=20 April 2021 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210420061553/https://www.bprol.com/en/2020/10/23/british-dominica-passport/}}</ref>

In 1660, the French and English agreed that Dominica and [[Saint Vincent (Saint Vincent and the Grenadines)|St. Vincent]] should not be settled, but instead left to the Carib people as neutral territory.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> However, its natural resources attracted expeditions of English and French foresters, who began harvesting timber.<ref name="bn">"Background note: Dominica". [[U.S. Department of State]] (July 2008). {{PD-notice}}</ref> In 1690, the French established their first permanent settlements. French woodcutters from [[Martinique]] and [[Guadeloupe]] began to set up timber camps to supply the French islands with wood, and they gradually became permanent settlers. They brought the first enslaved Africans from West Africa to ''Dominique'', as they called it in French.{{citation needed|date=June 2025}}

In 1715, a revolt of impoverished white smallholders in the north of Martinique, known as La Gaoulé,<ref>P.C. Emmer & BW Highman, (1999) ''General History of the Caribbean: Methodology and Historiography of the Caribbean,'' volume 6, [https://books.google.com/books?id=E12cpltazYgC&lpg=PA637 p. 637]</ref> caused settlers to migrate to southern Dominique, where they set up [[smallholding]]s. Meanwhile, French families and others from Guadeloupe settled in the north. In 1727, the first French commander, [[M. Le Grand]], took charge of the island with a basic French government. Dominique formally became a [[colony]] of France, and the island was divided into districts or "quarters".<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.lennoxhonychurch.com/article.cfm?Id=394 |title=Important Dates in Dominica's History |publisher=Lennox Honychurch |date=1990-07-05 |access-date=2013-09-29 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130830091704/http://www.lennoxhonychurch.com/article.cfm?Id=394 |archive-date=30 August 2013}}</ref> The French had already developed plantation agriculture on Martinique and Guadeloupe, where they cultivated [[sugarcane]] with enslaved African workers. In Dominique they gradually developed coffee plantations. Because of the trans-Atlantic slave trade, the general population came to consist primarily of black-African slaves.{{citation needed|date=June 2025}}

In 1761, during the [[Seven Years' War]] in Europe, a [[British expedition against Dominica]], led by [[Andrew Rollo, 5th Lord Rollo|Andrew Rollo]], conquered the island and several other Caribbean islands. In 1763, France lost the war and ceded the island to Great Britain in 1763 under the [[Treaty of Paris (1763)|Treaty of Paris]].<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> The same year, the British established a [[legislative assembly]], with only European colonists represented. French remained the official language, but [[Antillean Creole]], which had developed from it, was spoken by most of the population.{{citation needed|date=June 2025}}

In 1778 the French, with the active co-operation of the population, began the [[Invasion of Dominica (1778)|re-capture of Dominica]].<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> This was ended in 1783 by the [[Treaty of Paris (1783)|second Treaty of Paris]], which returned the island to British control, but the island population, especially those considered {{Lang|fr|gens de couleur libres}} ({{Lit|free people of colour}}), resisted British restrictions. The British retained control throughout French invasions in 1795 and 1805,<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/><ref name="bn" /> the first taking place during the period of the [[Haitian Revolution]], which gained the independence of Haiti (formerly Saint-Domingue, France's richest Caribbean colony).{{citation needed|date=June 2025}}

=== British colony ===
{{Main|British West Indies|British Leeward Islands|British Windward Islands}}

[[File:Agostino Brunias - Linen Market, Dominica - Google Art Project.jpg|thumb|A linen market in 1770s Dominica]]
Great Britain established a small colony in 1805. It used Dominica as part of the [[Atlantic slave trade|Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade]], by which slaves were imported and sold as labour in the islands as part of a trade that included producing and shipping sugar and coffee as commodity crops to Europe. The best documented slave plantation on the island is Hillsborough Estate, which had 71 male and 68 female slaves.  The [[Quarry Bank Mill|Greg]] family were notable: Thomas Hodgson, a brother-in-law, owned a slave ship, and Thomas Greg and his brother John Greg were part-owners of [[sugar plantation]]s in Dominica.  In January 1814, 20 slaves absconded from Hillsborough. They were recorded as recaptured and punished with 100 lashes applied to the males and 50 for the females. The slaves reportedly said that one of their people had died in the plantation hospital, and they believed he had been poisoned.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://janus.lib.cam.ac.uk/db/node.xsp?id=EAD/GBR/0115/RCMS+266|title=Janus: Dominica Estate documents|website=janus.lib.cam.ac.uk|access-date=19 September 2017|archive-date=20 September 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170920044851/https://janus.lib.cam.ac.uk/db/node.xsp?id=EAD%2FGBR%2F0115%2FRCMS%20266|url-status=live}}</ref>

In 1831, reflecting a liberalisation of official British racial attitudes, the Brown Privilege Bill<ref>{{cite book |url=https://archive.org/details/antislaverymont00englgoog |page=[https://archive.org/details/antislaverymont00englgoog/page/n279 211] |title=Anti-Slavery Monthly Reporter, volume 3|publisher=London Society for the Mitigation and Abolition of Slavery in the British Dominions |author=London Society for the Abolition of Slavery throughout the British Dominions|year=1831}}</ref> conferred political and social rights on [[free black]]s (mostly [[free people of colour]], who generally were of [[mixed race]], with African and European ancestry). With the [[Slavery Abolition Act 1833]], Britain ended the institution of slavery throughout [[British Empire|its empire]], except in India.<ref name=SectionLXIV>{{cite web |url=http://www.pdavis.nl/Legis_07.htm |title=Slavery Abolition Act 1833; Section LXIV |date=28 August 1833 |access-date=3 June 2008 |archive-date=24 May 2008 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080524010152/http://www.pdavis.nl/Legis_07.htm |url-status=live }}</ref>

With freedom came enfranchisement. In 1835, the first three men of African descent were elected to the legislative assembly of Dominica. Many slaves from the neighbouring French colonial islands of Guadeloupe and Martinique fled to Dominica. In 1838, Dominica became the first colony of the [[British West Indies]] to have an elected legislature controlled by an ethnic African majority. Most of these legislators had been free people of colour and smallholders or merchants before the abolition of slavery. Their economic and social views were different from the interests of the small, wealthy English [[plantation|planter]] class. Reacting to a perceived threat to their power, the planters lobbied for more direct British rule.<ref name=bn/>

In 1865, after much agitation and tension, the colonial office replaced the elective assembly with one made up of one-half members who were elected and one-half who were appointed. Planters, who were allied with colonial administrators, outmanoeuvred the elected legislators on many occasions. In 1871, Dominica became part of the [[British Leeward Islands]]. The political power of the elected assembly progressively eroded. [[Crown colony]] government was re-established in 1896.

[[File:Dominica stamps.jpg|thumb|Dominica stamps with portraits of [[George VI|King George VI]] and [[Elizabeth II|Queen Elizabeth II]]]]

In [[World War I]], many Dominicans, mainly the sons of small farmers, volunteered to fight in Europe for the British Empire. After the war, an upsurge of political consciousness throughout the Caribbean led to the formation of the [[List of historical autonomist and secessionist movements#British West Indies|Representative Government Association]]. Marshalling public frustration with the lack of a voice in the government of Dominica, this group won one-third of the popularly elected seats of the legislative assembly in 1924, and one-half in 1936. In 1940, administration of Dominica was transferred from the [[British Leeward Islands]] to the [[British Windward Islands]].<ref name=bn/> During [[World War II]], some Dominicans volunteered in British and Caribbean forces. Thousands of [[Free French]] refugees from Martinique and Guadeloupe escaped to Dominica from the [[Vichy France|Vichy]]-controlled French islands, staying in [[Roseau]] and other villages.

Until 1958, Dominica was governed as part of the [[British Windward Islands]]. Caribbean islands sought independence from 1958 to 1962, and Dominica became a province of the short-lived [[West Indies Federation]] in 1958.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/><ref name=bn/> After the federation dissolved in 1962, Dominica became an associated state of the United Kingdom in 1967, and formally took responsibility for its internal affairs.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> On 3 November 1978, Dominica was granted independence as a [[republic]], led by Prime Minister [[Patrick John]].<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/><ref name=bn/><ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/1978/1031/contents/made|title=The Dominica Termination of Association Order 1978 |website= legislation.gov.uk|archive-date=2 July 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180702011323/http://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/1978/1031/contents/made|url-status=live  |access-date=1 July 2018}}</ref>

=== Post-independence ===
In mid-1979, political discontent with founding prime minister Patrick John's administration climaxed in a civilian coup and ended in the passage of a [[motion of no confidence]] against John in the House of Assembly, Dominica's legislature, collapsing his administration. An Interim Government was formed under Dominica's second Prime Minister [[Oliver Seraphin]];<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> Seraphin's main task was to prepare the country for fresh general elections constitutionally due in 1980, hence the unofficial title "Interim Prime Minister". Seraphin organised and led a splinter of the Dominica Labour Party—the Democratic Labour Party—into the 1980 general election and lost mainly because his nearly 13 month-long premiership was dominated by the effects of Category Five [[Hurricane David]], which caused 56 deaths and untold damage across the island.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/><ref name="tcr3">{{cite web|url=http://www.nhc.noaa.gov/archive/storm_wallets/atlantic/atl1979-prelim/david/prelim03.gif|title=Hurricane David Preliminary Report, Page 3|last=Lawrence|first=Miles|year=1979|publisher=National Hurricane Center|access-date=5 October 2010|archive-date=9 March 2023|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230309235432/https://www.nhc.noaa.gov/archive/storm_wallets/atlantic/atl1979-prelim/david/prelim03.gif|url-status=live}}</ref> [[Hurricane Allen]] the following year caused further damage.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> After the [[1980 Dominican general election|1980 election]], Seraphin's government was replaced by one led by the [[Dominica Freedom Party]] (DFP) under Prime Minister [[Eugenia Charles]]; she was the Caribbean's first female prime minister.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/><ref name=":0">{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=nbGcAQAAQBAJ&q=eugenia+charles+pulmonary&pg=PA210|title=Oxford Dictionary of National Biography 2005–2008|last=Goldman|first=Lawrence|publisher= Oxford University Press|year=2013|isbn=9780199671540|pages=210|language=en}}</ref>

In 1981, Charles's government was threatened with two attempted coups. The first was led by [[Frederick Newton]], commander of the [[Military of Dominica]], who organised an attack on the police headquarters in Roseau which resulted in the death of a police officer.<ref name=coup>{{cite news|title=Ex-Commander Hanged For Dominica Coup Role|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1986/08/09/world/around-the-world-ex-commander-hanged-for-dominica-coup-role.html|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=9 August 1986|access-date=26 August 2009|archive-date=22 February 2012|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120222203135/http://www.nytimes.com/1986/08/09/world/around-the-world-ex-commander-hanged-for-dominica-coup-role.html|url-status=live}}</ref> Newton and five other soldiers were found guilty in the attack and sentenced to death in 1983; the sentences of the five accomplices were later commuted to life in prison, but Newton was executed in 1986.<ref name=coup/> A second occurred later in the year when the country was threatened with a takeover by [[mercenaries]],<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.country-data.com/cgi-bin/query/r-3376.html |title=Caribbean Islands – Regional Security Threats, 1970–81 |publisher=Country-data.com |access-date=2010-06-27 |archive-date=31 December 2016 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161231141341/http://www.country-data.com/cgi-bin/query/r-3376.html |url-status=live }}</ref> led by [[Mike Perdue]] and [[Wolfgang Droege]] in [[Operation Red Dog]]. They tried to overthrow Charles as prime minister and reinstate ex-prime minister John in exchange for control over the country's development. The [[Federal Bureau of Investigation|FBI]] was tipped off, and the ship hired to transport the mercenaries never left dock. The mercenaries lacked formal military experience or training, and most of the crew had been misled into joining by the ringleader Mike Perdue. [[White supremacy|White supremacist]] [[Don Black (white nationalist)|Don Black]] was jailed for his part in the attempted coup, which violated US [[Neutrality Act of 1794|neutrality]] laws.<ref>[https://www.amazon.com/Bayou-Pigs-Audacious-Tropical-Criminal/dp/0470153822 Stewart Bell, ''Bayou of Pigs''] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230811082328/https://www.amazon.com/Bayou-Pigs-Audacious-Tropical-Criminal/dp/0470153822 |date=11 August 2023 }}, presents the story of the planned coup.</ref>

[[File:President Ronald Reagan meets with Prime Minister Eugenia Charles.jpg|thumb|right|[[Eugenia Charles]], Prime Minister 1980–95, discussing the [[United States invasion of Grenada|situation in Grenada]] in 1983 with US President [[Ronald Reagan]]]]

The Charles government supported the 1983 American [[invasion of Grenada]], earning Dominica praise from the US government of [[Ronald Reagan]], and an increase in financial aid.<ref>Woodward, Bob, ''Veil: the Secret Wars of the CIA 1981–1987'', New York: Simon and Schuster, 1987, pp. 290, 300.<!-- ISSN/ISBN needed --></ref>

By the middle of the 1980s, the economy had begun to recover,<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> before weakening again due to a decrease in [[banana]] prices. Eugenia Charles won the [[1985 Dominican general election|1985]] general election, becoming the first incumbent Dominica Prime Minister to be popularly re-elected. The continuing downturn in the economy and the tight grip by Eugenia Charles on Dominica politics gave rise to a self-titled "Third Force" political formation in 1988, which disrupted the traditional two-party arrangement of governing DFP and opposition DLP. The formation soon formalised as the [[UWP (Dominica)|United Workers' Party]] (UWP) and selected as its leader [[Edison James]], the former general manager of the Dominica Banana Marketing Company. This was a strategic selection: he had prestige among banana farmers, and support from his origins in the East Coast, which had begun to feel alienated by the West Coast elites in Roseau, Dominica's capital.<ref name=bn/> Eugenia Charles again won the [[1990 Dominican general election|1990]] general election, the first incumbent Dominica prime minister to win three consecutive general elections, but the UWP's emergence had reduced her party's majority in Parliament to one. It was, therefore, no great surprise when Eugenia Charles gave up political leadership of the Dominica Freedom Party in 1993 and did not contest the 1995 general election in any capacity. No longer benefitting from her leadership, the Dominica Freedom Party lost the [[1995 Dominican general election|1995 election]] to the UWP, and James became prime minister.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> He attempted to diversify the Dominican economy away from over-reliance on bananas. The crop was largely destroyed by [[Hurricane Luis]] in 1995,<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> and James was unable to restore the banana industry to its former selling price and prestige. Moreover, his administration became embroiled in opposition charges of official corruption.

In the [[2000 Dominican general election|31 January 2000 general election]], the UWP were defeated by a coalition of the DLP, led by left-leaning [[Rosie Douglas|Roosevelt B. "Rosie" Douglas]], and the DFP led by former trade union leader [[Charles Savarin]]; Douglas became prime minister. One UWP member of the House of Assembly [[Crossing the floor|crossed the floor]], joining the DLP–DFP coalition government. Douglas died on 1 October 2000 after only a few months in office,<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mathaba.net/rosie/rosiedeath2.html|title= Rosie Douglas |website= mathaba.net|access-date=12 May 2018|archive-date=4 March 2016 |archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20160304053738/http://www.mathaba.net/rosie/rosiedeath2.html| url-status=usurped}}</ref> and was replaced by [[Pierre Charles (Dominican politician)|Pierre Charles]], who died in office on 6 January 2004.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/><ref>{{cite news |title=When Prime Minister Pierre Charles died |url=http://sundominica.com/articles/when-prime-minister-pierre-charles-died-4061/ |work=The Sun |access-date=7 October 2022 |archive-date=21 September 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200921005227/http://sundominica.com/articles/when-prime-minister-pierre-charles-died-4061/ |url-status=live }}</ref> [[Roosevelt Skerrit]], also of the DLP, replaced Pierre Charles as prime minister, becoming the world's youngest head of government at the age of 31.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> Under Skerrit's leadership, the DLP won [[2005 Dominican general election|elections in May 2005]] that gave the party 12 seats in the 21-seat Parliament. The UWP won 8 seats and Ronald Toulon, who was an independent candidate at that time, won the remaining seat. Later, Toulon joined the government.<ref name=bn/><ref>{{cite web |title=IFES Election Guide {{!}} Elections: Dominica Parliamentary May 5, 2005 |url=https://www.electionguide.org/elections/id/1412/ |website= electionguide.org |publisher= |access-date=7 October 2022 |archive-date=7 October 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221007213211/https://www.electionguide.org/elections/id/1412/ |url-status=live }}</ref> With his 2005 election win, Skerrit became only the second incumbent prime minister to be popularly re-elected.

In the [[2009 Dominican general election|2009 election]], the DLP won 18 of 21 seats. The UWP claimed campaign improprieties and embarked on a wide range of protest actions, including boycott of Parliament. UWP's boycott caused at least three unauthorised absences from Parliament for two of their three elected representatives in violation of Parliamentary procedure, leading to their two seats being declared vacant and by-elections called to fill them in July 2010 The UWP held both seats.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://2009-2017.state.gov/r/pa/ei/bgn/2295.htm |title=U.S. Department of State Background Note on Dominica |publisher=State.gov |date=2013-02-07 |access-date=2013-09-29 |archive-date=4 June 2019 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190604183830/https://2009-2017.state.gov/r/pa/ei/bgn/2295.htm |url-status=live }}</ref> The DLP under Skerrit went on to win the [[2014 Dominican general election]].<ref name=CE>[http://www.caribbeanelections.com/dm/elections/dm_results_2014.asp General Election Results – 8 December 2014] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190501051212/http://www.caribbeanelections.com/dm/elections/dm_results_2014.asp |date=1 May 2019 }} Caribbean Elections</ref>

On 17 September 2012 [[Eliud Williams|Eliud Thaddeus Williams]] was sworn in as president (a largely ceremonial role), replacing [[Nicholas Liverpool|Dr. Nicholas Liverpool]] who was reportedly removed from office due to ill health. On 30 September 2013 former trade union leader and former DFP leader [[Charles Savarin]] was elected president, having only days before resigned as a minister of government. He is Dominica's eighth president.<ref>{{cite news |title=Charles Angelo Savarin sworn in as Dominica's 8th President |url=http://news.gov.dm/news/1006-charles-angelo-savarin-sworn-in-as-dominicas-8th-president | website= news.gov.dm |publisher= Government of the State of Dominica |access-date=7 October 2022 |archive-date=7 October 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221007211648/http://news.gov.dm/news/1006-charles-angelo-savarin-sworn-in-as-dominicas-8th-president |url-status=live }}</ref>

[[File:Morning after Hurricane Maria (37372721465).jpg|thumb|right|Damage in [[Roseau]] caused by Hurricane Maria in 2017]]

[[Tropical Storm Erika]] devastated the island in August 2015, killing 30 and causing severe environmental and economic damage.<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/> Dominica was again struck on 18 September 2017, suffering a direct landfall from Category 5 [[Hurricane Maria]].<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica"/><ref>{{Cite news|title=Hurricane Maria 'devastates' Dominica: PM|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-latin-america-41317164|website=BBC News|access-date=19 September 2017|date=19 September 2017|archive-date=29 May 2023|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230529001401/https://www.bbc.com/news/world-latin-america-41317164|url-status=live}}</ref> Early estimates of damage suggested 90% of the buildings on the island had been destroyed, with infrastructure left in ruins.<ref>{{cite web|title=Dominica devastation emerges with fatalities and '90% of buildings destroyed'|url=http://wicnews.com/caribbean/dominica-devastation-emerges-fatalities-90-buildings-destroyed-44575828/|website=WIC News|access-date=19 September 2017|date=19 September 2017|archive-date=10 May 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200510110855/https://wicnews.com/caribbean/dominica-devastation-emerges-fatalities-90-buildings-damaged-44575828/|url-status=live}}</ref><ref name="Handy">{{Cite news|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-latin-america-41394645|title=Dominica grieves after Hurricane Maria|first=Gemma|last=Handy|date=25 September 2017|work=BBC News|access-date=21 July 2018|archive-date=21 July 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190721144022/https://www.bbc.com/news/world-latin-america-41394645|url-status=live}}</ref> The UK, France and the Netherlands set up shipping and air lifts to take aid to the island, the scale of destruction having left most people homeless.

Dominica won its first two Commonwealth Games medals in silver (men's triple jump) and bronze (women's triple jump) in the [[2018 Commonwealth Games]] on the [[Gold Coast, Queensland|Gold Coast]].<ref>{{cite web |title=DOC President Billy Doctrove says he is pleased with the National Team Commonwealth Games performance |url=https://www.q95da.com/news/doc-president-billy-doctrove-says-he-is-pleased-with-the-national-team-commonwealth-games-performance |website=Q95FM |language=en |access-date=7 October 2022 |archive-date=8 June 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230608192053/http://www.q95da.com/news/doc-president-billy-doctrove-says-he-is-pleased-with-the-national-team-commonwealth-games-performance |url-status=dead }}</ref>

President Charles Angelo Savarin was re-elected in 2018 for a new five-year term.<ref>{{cite news |title=DOMINICA-Savarin re-elected President, opposition stages walkout |url=https://antiguanewsroom.com/dominica-savarin-re-elected-president-opposition-stages-walkout/ |work=Antigua News Room |date=2 October 2018 |access-date=7 October 2022 |archive-date=13 February 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230213104803/https://antiguanewsroom.com/dominica-savarin-re-elected-president-opposition-stages-walkout/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

In December 2019, incumbent prime minister Roosevelt Skerrit won his fourth consecutive general [[2019 Dominican general election|election]] 18 seats to 3, becoming the first prime minister of Dominica to do so.<ref>{{cite news |title=Reflections on Dominica 2019 general election results |url=https://www.caribbeannewsglobal.com/reflections-on-dominica-2019-general-election-results/ |first=Rupert |last=Sorhaindo |work=Caribbean News Global |date=10 December 2019 |access-date=5 December 2021 |archive-date=7 June 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230607091947/https://www.caribbeannewsglobal.com/reflections-on-dominica-2019-general-election-results/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

Dominica received its first Olympic gold medal in the [[2024 Summer Olympics]] in [[Paris]] when [[Thea LaFond]] won the [[Athletics at the 2024 Summer Olympics – Women's triple jump|women's triple jump]] with a length of 15.02 metres.

== Government ==
{{main|Politics of Dominica}}

{{see also|Foreign relations of Dominica}}
<!---Please add new info to relevant articles (e.g. those linked immediately above) rather than here--->[[File:RooseveltSkerrit.jpg|thumb|The Prime Minister of the Commonwealth of Dominica, Roosevelt Skerrit]]
Dominica is a [[parliamentary democracy]] within the [[Commonwealth of Nations]].<ref name="English_constitution" /> The capital is [[Roseau]]. Dominica is one of the Caribbean's few [[republic]]s. The [[head of state]] is an indirectly elected [[President of Dominica|president]], while executive power rests with the [[Cabinet (government)|cabinet]], headed by the [[prime minister]].<ref name="English_constitution" /> The [[unicameral]] [[parliament]] consists of the 30-member [[House of Assembly of Dominica|House of Assembly]], which consists of 21 directly elected members, and 9 senators who may either be appointed by the president or elected by the other members of the House of Assembly.<ref name="English_constitution" />

Unlike other former British colonies in the region, Dominica has never been a [[Commonwealth realm]], having become a republic upon gaining its independence. Dominica is a full and participating member of the [[Caribbean Community|Caribbean Community (CARICOM)]] and the [[Organisation of Eastern Caribbean States]] (OECS).<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica" />

Dominica is also a member of the [[International Criminal Court]], with a [[Bilateral Immunity Agreement]] of protection with the US military, as covered under [[Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court|Article 98]]. In January 2008, Dominica joined the [[Bolivarian Alternative for the Americas]].<ref name="Encyclopedia Britannica - Dominica" />

=== Military ===
There has been no [[standing army]] in Dominica since 1981 when the [[Dominican Defence Force]] was disbanded following two violent coup attempts against  [[Eugenia Charles]].<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.demilitarisation.org/spip.php?article51&lang=en |title= Overview. 26 countries without armies |website= demilitarisation.org | publisher= Participative Institute for the Progress of Peace (APRED)| access-date= 2026-03-05}}</ref>

The civil [[Commonwealth of Dominica Police Force]] includes a Special Service Unit<ref>{{Cite web |title=Special Service Unit |url= http://cdpf.gov.dm/special-service-unit | website= cdpf.gov.dm |publisher= Commonwealth of Dominica Police Force |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210621124100/http://cdpf.gov.dm/special-service-unit |archive-date=21 June 2021 |access-date=21 June 2021 }}</ref> and [[Coast guard|Coast Guard]]. In the event of war or other emergency, if proclaimed by the authorities, the Police Force shall be a military force which may be employed for State defence (''Police Act'', Chapter 14:01).<ref>cited literally from ''[http://www.resdal.org/atlas/atlas10-ing-09-the-caribbean.pdf The Caribbean: Defence and Security in the Anglophone Caribbean — The Roads to Cooperation].'' in ''A Comparative Atlas of Defence in Latin America and Caribbean'', 2010 Edition, table ''Defence Force’s Mission'', p. 113 (pp. 113–128, pdf p. 1)</ref>

Dominica is a member of the [[Regional Security System]] (RSS).

== Economy ==
{{main|Economy of Dominica}}

Dominica's currency is the [[East Caribbean Dollar]]. In 2008, Dominica had one of the lowest [[per capita]] [[gross domestic product]] (GDP) rates of Eastern Caribbean states.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://2009-2017.state.gov/r/pa/ei/bgn/2295.htm|title=Dominica|website=U.S. Department of State|access-date=4 August 2020|archive-date=22 October 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201022010040/https://2009-2017.state.gov/r/pa/ei/bgn/2295.htm|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://siteresources.worldbank.org/INTOECS/Resources/Dominica.AAG.pdf|title=World Bank 'At A Glance'|access-date=4 February 2009|archive-date=3 March 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160303182822/http://siteresources.worldbank.org/INTOECS/Resources/Dominica.AAG.pdf|url-status=live}}</ref> The country nearly had a financial crisis in 2003 and 2004, but Dominica's economy grew by 3.5% in 2005 and 4.0% in 2006, following a decade of poor performance. Growth in 2006 was attributed to gains in tourism, construction, offshore and other services, and some sub-sectors of the banana industry. Around this time the [[International Monetary Fund]] (IMF) praised the Government of Dominica for its successful macroeconomic reforms, but also pointed out remaining challenges, including the need for further reductions in public debt, increased financial sector regulation, and market diversification.<ref name="bn" />

Agriculture, especially bananas, once dominated Dominica's economy,<ref name="CIA World Factbook - Dominica"/> and nearly one-third of the labour force worked in agriculture in the early 2000s. The sector, however, is highly vulnerable to weather conditions and to external events affecting commodity prices. In 2007, [[Hurricane Dean]] caused significant damage to the agricultural sector as well as the country's infrastructure, especially roads.{{citation needed|date=February 2020}} In response to reduced [[European Union]] (EU) [[trade preference]]s for bananas from the former European colonies after the 2009 [[WTO]] decision,<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/1999/mar/05/eu.wto3|title=The banana wars explained |first= Patrick |last= Barkham |newspaper= The Guardian|date=5 March 1999|access-date=4 August 2020 |archive-date=30 October 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171030040759/https://www.theguardian.com/world/1999/mar/05/eu.wto3|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/business-20263308|title=Banana war ends after 20 years|work=BBC News|date=8 November 2012|access-date=4 August 2020|archive-date=3 August 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200803211423/https://www.bbc.com/news/business-20263308|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=https://econlife.com/2018/06/banana-trade-war-2/ |title=How the EU and the U.S. Fought a Banana Trade War |website=Econlife |last=Schwartz |first=Elaine |date=26 June 2018 |access-date=16 July 2021 |url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190323103101/https://econlife.com/2018/06/banana-trade-war-2/ |archive-date=23 March 2019 }}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.europarl.europa.eu/news/en/headlines/world/20110121STO12285/ending-the-banana-wars-who-wins-and-who-loses|title=Ending the banana wars: Who wins and who loses? |publisher= European Parliament|date=24 January 2011|website= europa.eu|access-date=4 August 2020|archive-date=30 July 2020| archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20200730005518/https://www.europarl.europa.eu/news/en/headlines/world/20110121STO12285/ending-the-banana-wars-who-wins-and-who-loses|url-status=live}}</ref> the government has diversified the agricultural sector by promoting the production of coffee, [[patchouli]], [[aloe vera]], cut flowers, and exotic fruits such as mango, guava and papaya,{{citation needed|date=February 2020}} while the economy has become increasingly dependent on tourism.<ref name="CIA World Factbook - Dominica"/>

The expected increase of Dominica's [[Immigrant investor programs|Citizenship by Investment]] (CBI) fees has been suspended with no implementation date in sight, as announced by Prime Minister Roosevelt Skerrit, in the 2016 budget speech.<ref>[http://www.ntltrust.com/news/2016/7/28/no-fee-increase-for-dominica-cip No Fee Increase for Dominica CIP] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170917124313/http://www.ntltrust.com/news/2016/7/28/no-fee-increase-for-dominica-cip |date=17 September 2017 }}. ntltrust.com (2016-7-28)</ref>

=== International trade ===
Dominica is a beneficiary of the [[Caribbean Basin Initiative]] (CBI) that grants duty-free entry into the United States for many goods. Dominica also belongs to the predominantly English-speaking [[Caribbean Community]] (CARICOM), the [[CARICOM Single Market and Economy]] (CSME), and the [[Organisation of Eastern Caribbean States]] (OECS).<ref name="bn" />

=== Financial services industry ===
{{More citations needed section|date=January 2025}}
Dominica is becoming in recent years{{when|date=February 2020}} a major international financial centre. The largest sectors are "[[offshore banking]], [[payment processing]] companies, and general corporate activities".{{Quote without source|date=January 2025}} Regulation and supervision of the [[financial services]] industry is the responsibility of the [[Financial Service Unit of the Commonwealth of Dominica]] (FSU) under the supervision of the Ministry of Finance. There are a number of service providers. These include global financial institutions including [[Scotiabank]], [[Royal Bank of Canada]], [[Cathedral Investment Bank]], [[First Caribbean International Bank]], and The Interoceanic Bank of the Caribbean.

Starting in the mid-late 1990s, [[offshore financial centres]], such as Dominica, came under increasing pressure from the [[OECD]] for their allegedly harmful tax regimes, where the OECD wished to prevent low-tax regimes from having an advantage in the global marketplace. The OECD threatened to place Dominica and other [[financial centres]] on a "black list" and impose sanctions against them. Dominica successfully avoided being placed on the OECD black list by committing to regulatory reform to improve transparency and begin information exchange with OECD member countries about their citizens.

Dominica supposedly offers tax-free status to companies relocating from abroad. It is not known how many companies benefit from the tax-free status because of the strict confidentiality the government enforces, although it is known many Internet businesses and [[hedge fund]]s utilise Dominica for this reason. On 12 July 2012 Dominica signed an agreement with Poland to exchange tax information.<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.archbip.mf.gov.pl/bip/14227.html |title=Ministerstwo Finansów |publisher= mf.gov.pl |access-date=2013-09-29 |archive-date=21 January 2016 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160121004230/http://www.archbip.mf.gov.pl/bip/14227.html |url-status=live }}</ref>

=== Tourism ===
{{main|Tourism in Dominica}}

[[File:Batalie Bay, Dominica 002.jpg|thumb|Batalie Bay, Dominica]]

Dominica is mostly volcanic and has few beaches, contributing to [[Tourism in Dominica|tourism]] developing more slowly than on neighbouring islands.{{Citation needed|date=January 2025}} Nevertheless, Dominica's mountains, rainforests, freshwater lakes, hot springs, waterfalls, and diving spots make it an attractive [[ecotourism]] destination. Cruise ship stopovers have increased following the development of modern docking and waterfront facilities in Roseau, the capital.<ref name="bn" /> Out of 22 Caribbean islands tracked, Dominica had the fewest visitors in 2008 (55,800 or 0.3% of the total). This was about half as many as visited Haiti.<ref>{{cite news|title=Bookings started to fall along with stock market|date=19 January 2009| last1= DeLollis| first1=  Barbara| last2= Hansen| first2= Barbara|work= [[USA Today]]| access-date= }}</ref> The volcanic nature of the island has attracted [[scuba diving|scuba divers]].

== Infrastructure ==
=== Air ===
There are two airports currently operating on the island. The primary airport, [[Douglas-Charles Airport]] ([[IATA airport code]] DOM), has direct flights from [[Miami International Airport|Miami]] and [[Newark Liberty International Airport|Newark]]. It is on the northeast coast and is about a 45-minute drive from Portsmouth (1 hour from Roseau). Douglas-Charles Airport is suitable for limited use of commercial jets because of runway length. A runway extension and service upgrade project began at Douglas-Charles Airport around 2006 and was finished in 2010. A second airport, [[Canefield Airport]] (DCF), is about 15 minutes from Roseau on the southwest coast. A third, [[Dominica International Airport]], is currently under construction in [[Wesley, Dominica|Wesley]] and is expected to be completed in 2026.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Partners |first=CS Global |date=2023-09-07 |title=Dominica International Project Employs Over 200 |url=https://csglobalpartners.com/dominica-international-airport-project-employs-over-200-dominicans/ |access-date=2024-05-11 |website=CS Global Partners Limited |language=en-US}}</ref>

=== Roads ===
Dominica's road network runs primarily along the coastline and along river valleys. Major roads are two-lane highways which connect the capital, Roseau, with [[Portsmouth, Dominica|Portsmouth]] (the [[Edward Oliver LeBlanc|Edward Oliver Leblanc Highway]]) and Douglas Charles Airport (the Dr [[Nicholas Liverpool]] Highway). It takes about 45 minutes to drive from Portsmouth to Roseau. These major roads were reconstructed from the early 2010s to 2015 with assistance from the People's Republic of China and the European Union.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://dominicanewsonline.com/news/homepage/news/economy-development/completion-ceremony-of-edward-oliver-leblanc-highway-today/ |title=Completion ceremony of Edward Oliver Leblanc Highway Friday |date=20 April 2012 | work= Dominica News Online |access-date=2013-09-29 |archive-date=28 September 2013 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130928070103/http://dominicanewsonline.com/news/homepage/news/economy-development/completion-ceremony-of-edward-oliver-leblanc-highway-today/ |url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://news.gov.dm/index.php/news/1948-dr-nicholas-liverpool-highway-officially-commissioned|title=Dr Nicholas Liverpool Highway Officially Commissioned| website= news.gov.dm | publisher=Dominica Government Information Service|access-date=2015-10-04|archive-date=6 October 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151006233857/http://news.gov.dm/index.php/news/1948-dr-nicholas-liverpool-highway-officially-commissioned|url-status=live}}</ref> Private minibuses form the majority of the public transport system.{{Citation needed|date=January 2025}}

During [[Tropical Storm Erika#Effects in Dominica|Tropical Storm Erika]] in 2015, several road surfaces and bridges were damaged by flooding and landslides, including the just-completed Edward Oliver LeBlanc Highway (Roseau–Portsmouth) and Dr. Nicholas Liverpool Highway ([[Pont Cassé]]–Douglas Charles Airport). To alleviate this, the government announced that it intended to install emergency bridges in Roseau Valley near the Trafalgar Falls to Wotten Waven and in Emshall.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://dominicanewsonline.com/news/homepage/news/business/emergency-bridges-being-developed-pm-says/ |title=Emergency bridges being developed PM says |date=8 September 2015 |work= Dominica News Online |access-date=2015-10-04 |archive-date=4 June 2019 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190604123302/https://dominicanewsonline.com/news/homepage/news/business/emergency-bridges-being-developed-pm-says/ |url-status=live }}</ref> [[Hurricane Maria#Dominica|Hurricane Maria of 2017]] also damaged the road network.

=== Green energy ===
Dominica's electricity sector includes power from [[hydroelectricity]], [[solar energy]] and [[geothermal energy]].<ref>{{cite web |url= http://dominicanewsonline.com/news/homepage/news/business/emergency-bridges-being-developed-pm-says/|title=Emergency Bridges Being Developed PM Says|date=8 September 2015| work= Dominica News Online|access-date=5 October 2015|archive-date=28 August 2017|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20170828231021/http://dominicanewsonline.com/news/homepage/news/business/emergency-bridges-being-developed-pm-says/|url-status= dead}}</ref> After the devastation caused by [[Hurricane Maria]] in September 2017, the Dominican government claimed it would invest in geothermal energy. In early March 2018, Dominica signed an [[International Solar Alliance]] framework agreement, in an attempt to exploit solar energy to power the country with a source of renewable energy.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://renewableenergycaribbean.com/2018/08/24/dominica-cementing-its-position-as-a-renewable-energy-trailblazer/| title=Dominica cementing its position as a renewable energy trailblazer |first= |last= | website= renewableenergycaribbean.com |publisher= Caribbean Renewable Energy| date=24 August 2018|access-date=4 August 2020| archive-date=29 July 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200729232324/https://renewableenergycaribbean.com/2018/08/24/dominica-cementing-its-position-as-a-renewable-energy-trailblazer/|url-status=dead}}</ref>

The population of Dominica recorded in its census of June 1844 was 22,649.<ref>{{cite book |title=The National Cyclopaedia of Useful Knowledge |date=1848 |publisher=Charles Knight |location=London |page=502 | volume=V |edition=First}}</ref> 

Most Dominicans are of African descent.<ref name="britannica.com"/> There is a growing mixed population along with a small European-origin minority who are mostly descendants of French and British colonists and some people of Irish descent who were [[indentured servant]]s). A small number of [[Lebanese people|Lebanese]], [[Syrians]] and [[East Asian people|East Asians]] also reside on the island. Dominica is the only eastern Caribbean island that still has a population of pre-Columbian native [[Kalinago]] (previously called Caribs), who were exterminated or driven from neighbouring islands. {{As of|2014}} there are more than 3,000 Kalinago remaining, living in eight villages on the east coast of Dominica. This special [[Kalinago Territory]] (previously Carib Reserve) was granted by the British Crown in 1903.<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.avirtualdominica.com/caribs.htm |title=The Carib Indians |publisher= | website= avirtualdominica.com |archive-date=26 October 2010 |archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20101026103236/http://avirtualdominica.com/caribs.htm |url-status=live |access-date=2010-06-27}}</ref>

The population growth of Dominica is very slow, due primarily to immigration to other countries. <!-- Refers to Dominican Republic, not Dominica.  -->

Dominica has a relatively large number of [[centenarian]]s. In March 2007, there were 22 centenarians amongst the island's 70,000 inhabitants. This is three times the average incidence in developed countries. The reasons for this were studied at [[Ross University School of Medicine]].<ref name= "FOOC">{{cite episode |last= Pickford |first= John |series= [[From Our Own Correspondent]] |date= 31 March 2007 |publisher= [[BBC Radio 4]]| time= 17:49 – 22:55}}</ref>

Dominica was partially integrated into the federal colony of the [[Leeward Islands]] in 1832. In 1871, it became a full part of the Federation of the Leeward Islands. From the start it was a peculiar relationship, for previously Dominica had played no part in the political or cultural traditions of the other more Anglophone islands of the federation. As a Leeward Island, this much larger territory, with thousands of acres of forested unclaimed land, was open to the people of [[Montserrat]] and [[Antigua]]. At the beginning of the 20th century, the Rose's Company, which produced [[Rose's lime juice]], saw demand for its product outgrow its ability to supply the product from Montserrat. Their response to the situation was to buy land in Dominica and encourage Montserrat farm labourers to relocate. As a result, there emerged another linguistic community in Dominica, more specifically in [[Wesley, Dominica|Wesley]] and [[Marigot, Dominica|Marigot]], known as [[Kokoy]].

In 1902, on 8 May, the [[Mount Pelée]] volcano on Martinique erupted destroying the city of [[Saint-Pierre, Martinique|Saint-Pierre]]. Refugees from Martinique arrived in boats to the southern villages of Dominica and some remained permanently on the island.

=== Languages ===
English is the official language of Dominica, universally spoken and understood. In addition, [[Dominican Creole French|Dominican Creole]], a form of [[Antillean Creole]] based on [[French language|French]], is widely spoken. This originated from French migration to the island starting in 1690, a majority French Creole-speaking population that resided on the island,<ref>{{cite book|last1=Honychurch|first1=Lennox|title=The Dominica story : a history of the island|date=1995|publisher=Macmillan|location=London|edition= 3rd |isbn=978-0333627761| pages=49–60}}</ref> and its location between the two French-speaking departments of [[Martinique]] and [[Guadeloupe]]. Since 1979, Dominica has been a member of [[La Francophonie]]. Dominican Creole is particularly used among the older generation, which also speaks a [[patois]] language. Because of a decline in the use of Creole by the younger generation, initiatives have begun to increase usage and promote this unique part of the nation's history and culture.

Along with Creole, a dialect known as Kokoy (or Cockoy) is spoken.<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://www.amazon.co.uk/gp/reader/0521710162/ref=sib_dp_pt#reader-link| title=Lesser-known varieties of English|year=2010|publisher=Cambridge University Press|display-authors=etal|author=Schreier, D |isbn= 9780521710169 |archive-date=9 March 2021|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20210309030528/https://www.amazon.co.uk/gp/reader/0521710162/ref=sib_dp_pt#reader-link|url-status=live| access-date=30 August 2017}}</ref> It is a type of [[List of English-based pidgins|pidgin English]] which is a mix of [[Antiguan and Barbudan Creole]] and Dominican Creole,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.avirtualdominica.com/creole.htm|title=Creole for Beginners |publisher= | website= avirtualdominica.com|access-date=2010-06-27|archive-date=9 March 2020|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20200309172513/https://www.avirtualdominica.com/project/creole-kweyol-language/|url-status=live}}</ref> and is mainly spoken in the north-eastern villages of Marigot and Wesley, by the descendants of immigrants from [[Montserrat]] and [[Antigua]]. Over time there has been much intermarrying, but there are still traces of difference in origin.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.lennoxhonychurch.com/article.cfm?id=402|title=Inter-Island Migration and Cultural Change: The Impact of Montserratians on Dominica|first=Lennox|last= Honychurch|
website= lennoxhonychurch.com | publisher= Lennox Honychurch |date=14 November 2003|access-date=2010-06-27|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110511073901/http://www.lennoxhonychurch.com/article.cfm?id=402|archive-date=11 May 2011}}</ref> As a result of this mixture of languages and heritage, Dominica is a member of both the French-speaking Francophonie and the English-speaking [[Commonwealth of Nations]].

The [[Kalinago language]], also known as ''Igneri'' (also Iñeri, Igñeri, Inyeri), was an [[Arawakan language]] historically spoken by the [[Kalinago]] of the [[Lesser Antilles]] in the [[Caribbean]]. The Kalinago lived throughout the southern Lesser Antilles such as Dominica, [[Saint Vincent and the Grenadines|St Vincent]] and [[Trinidad and Tobago|Trinidad]], supposedly having conquered them from their previous inhabitants, the [[Igneri]]. Kalinago became extinct about 1920, but an offshoot survives as [[Garifuna language|Garifuna]], primarily in Central America.

=== Religion ===
{{main|Religion in Dominica}}

[[File:Roseau Virgin Ln - Our Lady of Fair Haven Cathedral - panoramio.jpg|thumb|[[Roseau Cathedral]] in Dominica]]
61.4% of the population is [[Catholic Church|Catholic]],<ref name="CIA World Factbook - Dominica" /> though in recent years a number of [[Protestantism|Protestant]] churches have been established.<ref>{{cite web |title=2022 Report on International Religious Freedom: Dominica |url=https://www.state.gov/reports/2022-report-on-international-religious-freedom/Dominica |website=state.gov |access-date=22 March 2026 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20250621022026/https://www.state.gov/reports/2022-report-on-international-religious-freedom/Dominica/ |archive-date=21 June 2025}}</ref> About 10–12% of the population belongs to one of the [[Seventh-day Adventist theology|Seventh-day Adventist]] denominations, which includes Yahweh Congregation, [[Church of God (Seventh-Day)]], and the [[Seventh-day Adventist Church]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.arabwashingtonian.org/english/article.php?articleID=547&issue=19|title=Tropical Islam| publisher= |website= arabwashingtonian.org |url-status=dead|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20090905155854/http://www.arabwashingtonian.org/english/article.php?articleID=547&issue=19|archive-date=5 September 2009 |access-date=2010-06-27}}</ref>

According to the [[Association of Religion Data Archives]], in 2010 the World Christian Database reported that the largest non-Christian religious groups included: [[Spiritualism (movement)|spiritualism]] followed by 2.6% of the population; [[Baháʼí Faith|Baháʼí]] followed by 1.7%; [[Buddhism]], [[Hinduism]], and [[Islam]], each followed by 0.1%; and [[Chinese folk religion]] and [[Neoreligion]]s were followed each one by a <0.1% of the population.

Non-believers ([[atheist]]s and [[Agnostic atheism|agnostics]]) are 6.1% of the population.<ref name="CIA World Factbook - Dominica"></ref>

The second largest town on the island, Portsmouth, is home to Al-Ansaar Masjid, the first mosque to be built in Dominica. The mosque was constructed with the help of Muslim students from the since-relocated [[Ross University School of Medicine]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=home |url=https://www.dominicamuslim.org/ |website=dominicamuslim.org| publisher= Muslim Community of Dominica |language=en-US |archive-date=1 December 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221201184401/https://www.dominicamuslim.org/ |url-status=live |access-date=2022-12-01}}</ref>

=== Education ===
{{main|Education in Dominica}}

{{More citations needed section|date=February 2016}}
Schooling in Dominica is mandatory up to secondary school. After pre-school, students attend primary school for six or seven years, and are admitted into secondary school on the basis of a Common Entrance Exam. After five years students take the Caribbean Secondary Education Certificate administered by the [[Caribbean Examinations Council|Caribbean Examination Council]] (a 15-member confederation of the Caribbean community (CARICOM)).<ref>{{Cite web |title= Dominica Education System |url=https://www.scholaro.com/db/Countries/Dominica/Education-System |access-date=2022-11-12 |website= scholaro.com |archive-date=12 November 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221112131856/https://www.scholaro.com/db/Countries/Dominica/Education-System |url-status= live}}</ref> The more advanced version of this examination, [[Caribbean Advanced Proficiency Examination|CAPE]], can be taken upon completion of two years of community college. The island has its own [[Dominica State College]], formerly named Clifton Dupigny Community College.<ref>{{Cite web |title=About Us |url= https://dsc.edu.dm/about-us/ |access-date=2022-11-12 |website= dsc.edu.dm| publisher= Dominica State College |language=en-US |archive-date=5 April 2023 |archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20230405102508/https://dsc.edu.dm/about-us/ |url-status=dead }}</ref> Some Dominicans attend universities in Cuba on scholarships offered by its government; others go to the [[University of the West Indies]] or to universities in the UK, US or other countries.

Archbold Tropical Research and Education Center is a biological field station owned by [[Clemson University]] and is located at Springfield Estate between [[Canefield]] and [[Pont Cassé]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.clemson.edu/public/rec/archbold/|title=Clemson University ATREC| year=2012| website= clemson.edu| publisher= Clemson University |access-date=2012-10-07|archive-date=7 November 2012|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20121107075155/http://www.clemson.edu/public/rec/archbold/|url-status=dead}}</ref> In 2006 [[All Saints University School of Medicine]] opened in temporary facilities in [[Loubière]],<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.allsaintsuniversity.org/|title= All Saints University School of Medicine, Dominica| website= allsaintsuniversity.org| publisher= All Saints University School of Medicine| access-date=2012-10-07|archive-date=18 August 2020|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20200818130626/http://www.allsaintsuniversity.org/|url-status=live}}</ref> it was later located in [[Roseau, Dominica]]. A [[marine biology]] institute in [[Mahaut, Dominica|Mahaut]], the Institute for Tropical Marine Ecology, closed in 2009.

[[Ross University School of Medicine]] was located at [[Portsmouth, Dominica|Portsmouth]]. Ross had been operating in Dominica since the 1980s.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rossu.edu/medical-school/|title=Ross University School of Medicine, Dominica| website= rossu.edu |archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20111204011700/http://www.rossu.edu/medical-school/|archive-date=4 December 2011|url-status=dead |access-date=2013-09-29}}</ref> Previously, a thousand medical students arrived each year from the US and Canada to study at Ross University, but the campus was permanently relocated to [[Barbados]] at the beginning of the 2019 Spring semester due to extensive hurricane damage suffered at the Dominican campus.<ref>{{cite web| url= https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/the_americas/medical-school-relocates-to-barbados-after-hurricane/2018/08/03/da1805e0-975f-11e8-818b-e9b7348cd87d_story.html?noredirect=on|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190421073939/https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/the_americas/medical-school-relocates-to-barbados-after-hurricane/2018/08/03/da1805e0-975f-11e8-818b-e9b7348cd87d_story.html%3Fnoredirect%3Don|url-status=dead|archive-date=21 April 2019|title=Medical school relocates to Barbados after hurricane |website= The Washington Post |access-date=3 August 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.adtalem.com/newsroom/press-releases/Adtalem-Global-Education-Announces-Barbados-as-New-Location-for-Ross-University-School-of-Medicine.html| title=Adtalem Global Education Announces Barbados as New Location for Ross University School of Medicine|publisher=Adtalem Global Education| website= adtalem.com| access-date=3 August 2018|archive-date=4 August 2018|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20180804014018/https://www.adtalem.com/newsroom/press-releases/Adtalem-Global-Education-Announces-Barbados-as-New-Location-for-Ross-University-School-of-Medicine.html|url-status=dead}}</ref>

The Dominica Library and Information Service (DLIS) serves an integral role in the education of its citizens, provides service for the population of Dominica through three components: public library services, documentation and research services, and archival services.<ref name= About>{{Cite web |title= About DLIS |url=https://dlis.gov.dm/about-dlis |website=dlis.gov.dm | publisher= Dominica Library and Information Service | date= |language=en-gb |access-date=2022-12-01}}</ref> The institution was established in 1843 with the opening of reading rooms, the first public library in Dominica, Victoria Memorial.<ref name= About /> What began as a reading room evolved into public library that wasn't fully free: patrons were required to pay a subscription fee. Surviving religious and political discord, the Great Depression, and two world wars, the library finally came under the care of the government where funds were set aside for its upkeep. It is managed by the Ministry of Education and Human Resource Development.<ref name= About /> Ironically, the early stages of the library's history were dedicated to remove the "uncouth", and "barbarous patois", which is being preserved. However, it did achieve its goal of "diminishing the island’s very high percentage of illiteracy".<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Boromé|first=Joseph A.|date=1970|title=Origin and Growth of the Public Libraries of Dominica|journal= [[The Journal of Library History]] |volume=5|issue=3|page=225 |jstor=25540240|issn=0022-2259}}</ref> The historical library was demolished in the wake of [[Hurricane Maria]] in 2018 and is slated to be rebuilt with a more modern outlook.

== Culture ==
{{main|Culture of Dominica|Music of Dominica}}

[[File:Carib Territory.svg|thumb|Dominica's east coast [[Carib Territory]]]]

Dominica was historically occupied by the [[Arawak peoples|Arawaks]] (Tainos) and Carib (Kalinago) tribes at the time European settlers reached the island. "[[Massacre River (Dominica)|Massacre]]" is a name of a river dedicated to the [[Kalinago Genocide of 1626|mass murder of the native villagers]] by English settlers on St. Kitts. The survivors were forced into exile on Dominica.<ref name=Hubbard>{{cite book|last1=Hubbard|first1=Vincent|title=A History of St. Kitts|date=2002|publisher=Macmillan Caribbean|isbn=9780333747605|page=[https://archive.org/details/historyofstkitts00vinc/page/17 17]|url-access=registration|url=https://archive.org/details/historyofstkitts00vinc/page/17}}</ref> Both the French and British claimed the island and imported [[slaves]] from Africa for labour. The remaining Kalinago live on a {{convert|3700|acre|km2|0|adj=on}} territory on the east coast of the island. They elect their own chief. This has produced the modern cultural mix.{{Original research inline|date=February 2021}}

Music and dance are important facets of Dominican culture. The annual independence celebrations display a variety of traditional song and dance. Since 1997, there have also been weeks of Creole festivals, such as "Creole in the Park" and the "[[World Creole Music Festival]]".{{Clarify|date=May 2025}}

Dominica gained prominence on the international music stage in 1973, when [[Gordon Henderson (musician)|Gordon Henderson]] founded the group [[Exile One]] and an original musical genre, which he called "[[Cadence-lypso]]" was born. This paved the way for modern Creole music. Other musical genres include "[[Jing ping]]" and "[[Cadence rampa|Cadence]]". Jing ping features the accordion and is native to the island. Dominica's music is a mélange of Haitian, Afro-Cuban, African and European traditions. Popular artists over the years included Chubby and the Midnight Groovers, Bells Combo, [[the Gaylords (Dominican band)|The Gaylords]], [[Windward Caribbean Kulture|WCK]], and Triple Kay.

The 11th annual World [[creole music|Creole Music]] Festival was held in 2007, part of the island's celebration of independence from Great Britain on 3 November. A year-long reunion celebration began in January 2008, marking 30 years of independence.

Dominica is often seen as a society that is migrating from [[Collectivism and individualism|collectivism]] to individualism. The economy is a developing one that previously depended on agriculture. Signs of collectivism are evident in the small towns and villages which are spread across the island.{{clarify|date=February 2020}}

The novelist [[Jean Rhys]] was born and raised in Dominica. The island is obliquely depicted in her best-known book, ''[[Wide Sargasso Sea]]''. Rhys's friend, the political activist and writer [[Phyllis Shand Allfrey]], set her 1954 novel, ''[[The Orchid House (novel)|The Orchid House]]'', in Dominica.

Much of the [[Walt Disney Pictures|Walt Disney]] film ''[[Pirates of the Caribbean: Dead Man's Chest]]'' (the second in the series, released in 2006), was shot on location on Dominica (though in the film it was known as "Pelegosto", a fictional island), along with some shooting for the third film in the series, ''[[Pirates of the Caribbean: At World's End|At World's End]]'' (2007).

=== Cuisine ===
{{main|Dominica cuisine}}

Dominica's cuisine is similar to that of other Caribbean islands, particularly Jamaica, Saint Lucia, and Trinidad and Tobago. Like other [[Commonwealth Caribbean]] islands, Dominicans have developed a distinct twist to their cuisine. Breakfast is an important daily meal, typically including saltfish, dried and salted [[codfish]], and "bakes" (fried dough). Saltfish and bakes are combined for a fast food snack that can be eaten throughout the day; vendors on Dominica's streets sell these snacks to passersby, together with fried chicken, fish and fruit and yogurt "smoothies". Other breakfast meals include cornmeal [[porridge]], which is made with fine cornmeal or ''polenta'', milk or condensed milk, and sugar to sweeten. Traditional British-influenced dishes, such as eggs and toast, are also popular, as are fried fish and [[Plantain (cooking)|plantains]].{{Citation needed|date=May 2025}}

Common vegetables include [[Plantain (cooking)|plantains]], [[Xanthosoma sagittifolium|tannia]]s (a root vegetable), [[sweet potatoes]], [[potatoes]], [[rice and peas]]. Meat and poultry typically eaten include chicken, beef and fish. These are often prepared in stews with onions, carrots, garlic, ginger and herbs. The vegetables and meat are browned to create a rich dark sauce. Popular meals include rice and peas, [[brown stew chicken]], stew beef, fried and stewed fish, and many different types of hearty fish broths and soups{{Citation needed|date=May 2025}}. These are filled with dumplings, carrots and ground provisions.

=== Sports ===
[[Cricket]] is a popular sport on the island, and Dominica competes in [[test cricket]] as part of the [[West Indies cricket team]]. In West Indies domestic first-class cricket, Dominica participates as part of the [[Windward Islands cricket team]], although they are often considered a part of the [[Leeward Islands]] geographically. This is due to being part of the [[British Windward Islands]] colony from 1940 until independence; its cricket federation remains a part of the Windward Islands Cricket Board of Control.

On 24 October 2007, the 8,000-seat [[Windsor Park (Dominica)|Windsor cricket stadium]] was completed with a donation of [[EC$]]33&nbsp;million ([[US$]]17&nbsp;million, [[euro|€]]12&nbsp;million) from the [[government of the People's Republic of China]].

[[Netball]], [[basketball]], [[Rugby football|rugby]], [[tennis]] and [[association football]] are gaining popularity.

International footballer [[Julian Wade]], Dominica's all-time top goal scorer as of 2021, plays for [[Brechin City F.C.]] in Scotland.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.thecourier.co.uk/fp/sport/football/2451688/dominica-international-julian-wade-brechin-city/|title=Dominica international striker Julian Wade gives up Caribbean sunshine to hit Brechin City goal trail|date=2021-08-12|author=Smith, Ewan|publisher=The Courier|access-date=3 January 2022|archive-date=3 January 2022|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220103175840/https://www.thecourier.co.uk/fp/sport/football/2451688/dominica-international-julian-wade-brechin-city/|url-status=live}}</ref>

During the [[2014 Winter Olympics]], a husband and wife team of Gary di Silvestri and Angela Morrone di Silvestri spent [[US$]]175,000 to register as Dominican citizens and enter the 15&nbsp;km men's and 10&nbsp;km women's [[Cross-country skiing at the 2014 Winter Olympics|cross-country skiing]] events, respectively. Angela did not start her race, and Gary pulled out several hundred meters into his race. {{as of|2022}}, they are Dominica's only Winter Olympic athletes.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://deadspin.com/dominicas-fake-ski-team-scammed-the-olympics-and-the-p-1529973935|title=Dominica's Fake Ski Team Scammed The Olympics and the Press|date=2014-02-24|publisher=Deadspin|author=McKenna, Dave|access-date=2014-02-25|archive-date=26 February 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140226153358/http://deadspin.com/dominicas-fake-ski-team-scammed-the-olympics-and-the-p-1529973935|url-status=live}}</ref>

Athlete [[Jérôme Romain]] won the bronze medal at the [[1995 World Championships in Athletics]] [[1995 World Championships in Athletics – Men's triple jump|triple jump competition]]. He also qualified for the finals at the [[Athletics at the 1996 Summer Olympics – Men's triple jump|1996 Olympic Games]]; even though he had to pull out due to injury, until 2024 his 12th position was the best ever performance of a Dominican at the [[Olympic Games|Olympics]].<ref>{{cite web |title=World bronze medallist Romain reflects on Dominica's Olympic debut |url=https://trackalerts.com/world-bronze-medallist-romain-reflects-on-dominicas-olympic-debut/ |website=trackalerts.com |date=15 August 2016 |access-date=14 February 2022 |archive-date=15 February 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220215014515/https://trackalerts.com/world-bronze-medallist-romain-reflects-on-dominicas-olympic-debut/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

[[sprint (running)|Sprinter]] Olympian [[Chris Lloyd]] won the bronze medal at the [[2007 Pan American Games]] over [[400m]].<ref>{{cite news |title=Dominica's Participation at Beijing 2008 Olympic Games |url=http://www.dominica-weekly.com/news/dominicas-participation-at-beijing-2008-olympic-games/ |access-date=14 February 2022 |publisher=dominicaweekly |archive-date=5 March 2016 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160305030723/http://www.dominica-weekly.com/news/dominicas-participation-at-beijing-2008-olympic-games/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

[[Triple Jump]] Olympian [[Thea LaFond]] became the first athlete to win a medal at the [[2018 Commonwealth Games]]<ref>{{cite web |title=Thea Lafond Records World Leading Jump |url=https://dominicaathleticsassociation.org/index.php/2021/01/24/thea-lafond-records-world-leading-jump/ |website=dominicaathleticsassociation.org |date=24 January 2021 |access-date=14 February 2022 |archive-date=12 August 2021 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210812120835/https://dominicaathleticsassociation.org/index.php/2021/01/24/thea-lafond-records-world-leading-jump/ |url-status=live }}</ref> and an Olympic medal, earning gold at the [[2024 Summer Olympics]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.reuters.com/sports/olympics/athletics-lafond-wins-triple-jump-gold-bring-dominica-first-ever-olympic-medal-2024-08-03/|title=LaFond wins triple jump gold to bring Dominica first ever Olympic medal|first=Helen|last=Reid|date=3 August 2024|work=Reuters|access-date=3 August 2024}}</ref>

== Media ==
Dominica has two major newspapers, ''[[The Sun (Dominica newspaper)|The Sun]]''<ref>{{Cite news |url=http://sundominica.com/ |title=Breaking News, Caribbean, Dominica News & Analysis &#124; the Sun |access-date=7 October 2022 |archive-date=7 October 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221007213506/http://sundominica.com/ |url-status=live }}</ref> and ''[[The Chronicle (Dominica)|The Chronicle]]'', and two national television stations. Radio stations include the [[Dominica Broadcasting Corporation]] and independent stations come and go. [[Digicel]] and [[Flow (brand)|FLOW]] operate mobile phone services on the island for the use of Dominica's wireless customers. Historical newspapers include ''The Dominican'',  ''The Dominica Guardian'',{{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210129121314/https://dloc.com/AA00079437/00003/allvolumes |date=29 January 2021 }}, and the ''Dominica Colonist'', available for free in the [[Digital Library of the Caribbean]].
